Overview

Human-like touch sensing is challenging: human skin spans a wide hardness range (≈2A–20A Shore), integrates thousands of receptors/cm², and endures millions of cycles without tearing. Most robotic skins fail by being too rigid, too low density, or too fragile. Kind Humanoid set out to build a multilayer skin system with realistic mechanical response and scalable sensing integration.

The approach: a Dragon Skin FX Pro outer shell for toughness and skin-like feel, a Flex-Foam VIII core tuned for elasticity and compliance, and embedded electrodes for capacitive or resistive readout. Beyond fabrication, the project emphasized repeatable workflows—molds, mixing ratios, demolding protocols—that could scale.

Highlights: Optimized foam mix (1:1.6 A:B) for structure; syringe-injection silicone molds for bubble-free 1 mm sheets; iterative capacitive/resistive stacks to study hysteresis, latency, and wiring-induced shorts.

Manufacturing

Silicone Layer — Dragon Skin FX Pro (1 mm)

We used 10×10 cm plates with a threaded luer-lock port and 4 vents. Degassing was mandatory: without vacuum, bubbles formed and weakened the sheet. With slow injection and vent weeping, 1 mm sheets cured consistently.

Failure modes: plunger pressed too fast → side leakage; insufficient release spray → tearing; rushed demold → edge fractures.

  1. Prep: Tap port; clean; light release on wet surfaces; clamp plates; attach luer & 20 mL syringe.
  2. Mix: 1:1 (A:B), e.g., 5 mL + 5 mL; pre-mix ~2 min each addition.
  3. Degas: Vacuum ~3–4 min until bubbling subsides; vent slowly.
  4. Inject: Funnel → syringe; slow plunger for laminar fill until vents weep; wipe excess.
  5. Cure: ~40 min at ≥18 °C; unclamp; free edges; pry at tabs; peel sheet carefully.
  6. Cleanup: IPA wipe; water rinse; tooling reusable.
Foam Layer — Flex-Foam VIII (porous elastic)

Default 1:2 ratio produced tacky, dense sheets. We empirically optimized to 1:1.6 A:B, yielding a non-tacky porous foam with good compliance. Heavy release spray caused crystallized surfaces—only a light mist worked. Foam expanded ≈7×, so liquid volume had to be carefully planned.

Safety: Part B is hazardous—gloves, goggles, respirator required.

Materials Reasoning

Dragon Skin FX Pro (outer shell)
  • Durability: high toughness; tensile strength ~50 MPa; fatigue resistant.
  • Deformability: Shore ≈ 2A; elongation up to ~763% (epidermis-like feel).
  • Thermal/Chemical: −60 °C → 230 °C; inert, skin-safe (platinum-cure).
  • Manufacturability: ~40 min cure; injection-mold friendly; scalable.
Flex-Foam VIII (compliance layer)
  • Density 8 lb/ft³; extremely lightweight, improves dexterity.
  • Low modulus → large elastic deformation, distributes pressure evenly.
  • Optimized ratio 1:1.6 produced uniform, non-tacky sheets.
  • Dielectric properties enable use as part of capacitive stack.

Design Iterations

Shear-Sensing Nib Arrays

Each nib houses four electrodes to resolve normal + shear. Arrays up to 20×20 on 10×10 cm (~16 receptors/cm²) approach human fingertip densities (~10/cm²). Limitation: routing four electrodes per nib without crosstalk at our fab scale.

Finger Wraps with Strain-Relief

Early wraps wrinkled and tore at joints. Revised designs added strain-relief “wings” and side flaps for modular fit. Two design paths were tested: one emphasizing tight fit and PCB adhesion, another prioritizing flexibility. Final: silicone wraps gave better thickness control for electronics tests.

Electronics & Schematics

Capacitive Stack & PCB
  • Sensors: Exposed touchpad matrix; orthogonal row/column pads (grid addressing).
  • Frontend: Analog mux → capacitive ADC (24-bit class) → RP2040 acquisition.
  • Power/Comm: LDO regulation; RS-485 bus for robust multi-drop comms.
  • Layout: Sensor area isolation; ground/guarding to reduce coupling.
Resistive Stack (Velostat) & PCB
  • Velostat sheet between copper electrodes; resistance read via ADC (ESP32 in early builds).
  • Pros: simple readout. Cons: shorts at flex points; pressure distribution sensitivity.
  • Isolation, strain-relief, and guard traces are critical for stability.

Testing

Protocol
  1. Assemble stack; connect to Arduino/RP2040 logger; zero baselines.
  2. Apply forces at labeled points (10 g, 25 g, 50 g, 100 g) for ~2 s each; 3 trials per point.
  3. Record time-series from all channels; annotate force level & location.
  4. Evaluate rise time, drift, hysteresis (loading vs unloading), spatial selectivity, and noise.
Observations
  • Capacitive stacks detect force but exhibit latency and mild hysteresis (~±1 unit).
  • Resistive fingers show intermittent shorts at bends; wiring path and strain-relief matter.
  • Resolution improves with denser grids; routing + shielding become more complex.

Prototype Timeline

  1. P1 — Shear nib grid: promising shear cues; abandoned due to electrode density constraints.
  2. P2 — Rough capacitive: rigid lab foam dielectric; partial success, moved to custom foam.
  3. P3 — Layered silicone + foam: force detected; delayed response + hysteresis.
  4. P4 — Resistive fingers (Velostat): inconsistent due to shorts and handling sensitivity.
  5. P5 — Revised capacitive with Velostat layer: improved force pickup; limited sensitivity <≈ 30 N.

Results & Next Steps

  • Repeatable manufacturing workflow for thin silicone sheets and compliant foam layers.
  • CAD for molds, finger wraps with strain-relief, and high-density nib arrays.
  • Capacitive & resistive prototypes characterized (latency, hysteresis, failure modes).
  • Next: guard traces & shielding; FPC tails at joints; faster ADC/AFE; explore stretchier nib materials.
